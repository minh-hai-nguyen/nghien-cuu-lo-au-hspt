Abstract
Background: Social anxiety disorder (SAD) significantly affects young individuals' social and academic functioning,
emphasising the need for accessible and effective treatments such as digital mental health interventions (DMHI).
Objective: This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to evaluate the efficacy of DMHI for children, adolescents and
young adults with social anxiety.
Methods: For review and meta-analysis databases were searched for randomised controlled trials investigating DMHI
addressing social anxiety in young people (mean age <25 years). Two authors independently screened the records, extracted data
and assessed the Risk of Bias.
Results: This systematic review includes k=22 and the meta-analysis k=21 studies. Results significantly favoured DMHIs
(g=0.508; 95% CI: 0.308; 0.707) over any control condition (i.e., waitlist or active interventions) after the intervention,
specifically those compared to waitlist control conditions (g=0.576; 95% CI: 0.343; 0.809), based on cognitive behavioural
principles (g=0.610; 95% CI: 0.361; 0.859), incorporating SAD-specific components (g=0.878; 95% CI: 0.469; 1.278) and those
with human guidance (g=0.825; 95% CI: 0.425; 1.224). Neither parental involvement nor age influenced outcomes significantly.
When publication bias was considered, the overall effect remained significant (g=0.506 (95% CI= 0.308; 0.707).
Conclusions: This meta-analysis provides support for the efficacy of DMHI for social anxiety compared to no intervention and
the beneficial effects of guidance and interventions specifically designed for SAD. Further, it highlights methodological
shortcomings and heterogeneous reporting standards. Future research should prioritise higher methodological quality and should
explore how effects are related to age and specific intervention components, including guidance and treatment modules. Clinical
Trial: This systematic review and meta-analysis was preregistered on PROSPERO (ID: CRD42023424181).

Abstract
Background:  Social anxiety disorder (SAD) significantly affects young individuals'
social and academic functioning, emphasising the need for accessible and effective
treatments such as digital mental health interventions (DMHI). 
Objective: This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to evaluate the efficacy of
DMHI for children, adolescents and young adults with social anxiety.
Methods:  For  review  and  meta-analysis  six  electronic  databases  (APA PsycINFO,
Embase, MEDLINE, PsyIndex, PubMed, Web of Science) were searched for randomised
controlled trials investigating DMHI addressing social anxiety in young people (mean
age <25 years). Two authors independently screened the records, extracted data and
assessed the Risk of Bias. For data analysis, a standardised effect size was calculated
using Hedges’ g, along with 95% confidence intervals for each study. Meta-analyses were
conducted using a random-effects model to account for heterogeneity.
Results:  This systematic review includes  k=22 and the meta-analysis  k=21 studies.
Results significantly favoured DMHIs (g=0.508; 95% CI: 0.308; 0.707) over any control
condition (i.e., waitlist or active interventions) after the intervention, specifically those
compared to waitlist control conditions (g=0.576; 95% CI: 0.343; 0.809), based on
cognitive behavioural principles (g=0.610; 95% CI: 0.361; 0.859), incorporating SAD-
specific components (g=0.878; 95% CI: 0.469; 1.278) and those with human guidance
(g=0.825; 95% CI: 0.425; 1.224). Neither parental involvement nor age influenced
outcomes  significantly.  When  publication  bias  was  considered,  the  overall  effect
remained significant (g=0.506; 95% CI= 0.308; 0.707). Risk of bias assessment indicated
that most studies showed some concerns, three studies were classified as high risk and
three were rated as low risk. The reporting of adherence varied greatly and could not be
analysed meta-analytically. 
Conclusion:  This meta-analysis provides support for the efficacy of DMHI for social
anxiety  compared  to control  conditions  and  the  beneficial  effects  of guidance  and
interventions  specifically  designed  for  SAD.  Further,  it  highlights  methodological
shortcomings and heterogeneous reporting standards. Future research should prioritise
higher methodological quality and should explore how effects are related to age and
specific intervention components, including guidance and treatment modules.
Trial Registration: This systematic review and meta-analysis was  preregistered on
PROSPERO (ID: CRD42023424181).

Introduction
Social Anxiety Disorder in Children, Adolescents and Young Adults
Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental disorders in children, adolescents and young adults
[1–3]. Especially in adolescence, social anxiety disorder (SAD) is highly relevant, with prevalence
rates between 12 to 36% [4,5]. Notably, 88% of SAD cases have their onset before the age of 25
years and 50% before 14 years [6]. The first symptoms (e.g., shyness, fear of embarrassment) often
appear in childhood [7,8] and in adolescence up to 50% report subclinical levels of social anxiety
symptoms [9,10]. 
SAD is characterised by an intense and persistent fear of embarrassing oneself in front of others or
being judged negatively by others [11]. To endure feared situations, those affected by SAD often
engage in safety behaviours to minimise negative evaluations or avoid social situations altogether
[11].  These  behaviours  maintain  social  anxiety  symptoms  [12–14],  contribute  to  substantial
impairment in their psychosocial functioning (e.g., fewer friends than their healthy peers) and the
development of comorbid disorders (e.g., depression, substance use) and lead to negative long-term
effects, such as poorer academic and job opportunities and lack of social support due to a small or
non-existent social network [15–20]. These detrimental consequences demand prevention efforts and
effective treatments to intervene early. 
Efficacy of Treatment and Barriers to Treatment
Established treatment protocols (e.g., based on the Cognitive Model by Clark and Wells  [12]) in
adults have been adapted to suit the specific needs of children and adolescents [21]. Meta-analyses
support their efficacy in individual and group settings compared to waitlist or psychological placebo
control  groups  (i.e.,  attention  control)  [22–24].  Accordingly,  individual  or  group  cognitive
behavioural therapy (CBT) is recommended as a first-line treatment for children and adolescents
with SAD [25,26]. 
Although effective treatments exist, treatment delivery and uptake are impeded by poor mental
health literacy (e.g., poor symptom recognition), public- and self-stigma, particularly in those with
SAD, and a gap in the provision of care [27–30]. The care gap is even more exacerbated in children
and adolescents, in ethnic minority groups, those living in rural areas or in lower-income countries
and those with lower socio-economic status [31–33]. Various efforts have addressed some of these
barriers  by  integrating  care  in  primary  physician  settings,  implementing  psychoeducational
interventions  in  educational settings, or by task sharing  (e.g., non-professional people  deliver
interventions under supervision) [34–36]. Digital interventions are a highly scalable and far-reaching
solution that represents another promising option that may circumvent some of the barriers not
addressed in the previously mentioned efforts (i.e., time and place independence). 
Digital Mental Health Interventions for Social Anxiety
Digital mental health interventions (DMHI) aim to manage, alleviate, or treat mental health problems
through a digital medium (i.e., internet, mobile phone application (app), wearables, SMS) [37,38].
They  can  include  some form of human  support (i.e., guided self-help),  for instance,  through
asynchronous messages or synchronous real-time chatting or phone calls by a professional, or they
can be completely standalone (i.e., unguided self-help) [39]. Several DMHIs have been developed
for adult  SAD, with the evidence base  for SAD  being  among  the most extensive, including
interventions based on CBT, psychodynamic therapy, interpersonal therapy and acceptance-based
CBT [39,40]. For this age group, meta-analytic evidence supports the efficacy of DMHIs as internet-
based CBT (ICBT) and virtual reality exposure significantly outperformed passive or waitlist control
conditions. Compared to other active control conditions, ICBT yielded small effects and showed to
be equally efficacious as face-to-face psychotherapy [41–43].

For DMHIs for the prevention of anxiety disorders, meta-analyses did not yield significant overall
effects, but some RCTs found small effects in favour of DMHIs [48,49]. Likewise, meta-analyses of
prevention programmes for anxiety disorders in school-based settings reported small to medium
effect sizes compared to passive control conditions after the intervention [50,51]. 
Notably, some studies  suggest that young people with  SAD  benefit  less  from transdiagnostic
interventions that target several anxiety disorders than young people with other anxiety disorders
[52–54].  However other studies found no difference between SAD-specific and transdiagnostic
anxiety interventions for children and adolescents with SAD [55,56]. Thus, it would be relevant to
compare the effects of DMHIs that specifically target SAD with those that target anxiety disorders in
general. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, so far, no systematic review and meta-analysis has
investigated the effect of DMHI on social anxiety in children and adolescents specifically. 
Moderators in Treatment Outcome
DMHIs are not equally effective for all children, adolescents and young adults. The subsequent
section will discuss potential moderators of treatment efficacy, which include control conditions,
psychological principles of the interventions, age, adherence and the extent of support. Generally, the
effect sizes are larger when DMHIs are compared with inactive control conditions (e.g., waiting list)
than with active control conditions [57,58]. Most DMHIs developed are based on CBT principles
[37] though when compared to DMHIs based on other psychological interventions (e.g., Cognitive
bias modification (CBM), mindfulness) they do not result in better effects [59]. Evidence concerning
age shows that older adolescents and young adults generally benefit more from DMHIs than children
[46,60,61].  Similarly, adherence is supposed to be linked to symptom improvement, reflecting
compliance with a predefined and recommended use and engagement with the intervention [62–64].
However, results regarding the association between adherence and outcome are more complex. Some
studies point to a direct link between adherence and symptom improvement [65], some to an inverse
relationship [66] and others identify only single aspects of adherence (e.g., module completion) to be
relevant for symptom improvement [67]. Furthermore, studies indicate that adherence itself is, in
turn, influenced by symptom severity, which may thereby affect symptom reduction [62]. Overall,
adherence plays an important role in implementing DMHIs, and human support is a known factor in
improving adherence [68,69]. This support can be provided in the form of guidance by professionals
(i.e., psychologists and therapists) or by the social context of the young people (i.e., parents and
teachers). 
Objectives of this Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
DMHIs are especially promising in young people as they are digital natives and generally seek help
later and to a lesser extent than adults [38,70,71]. Thereby, the low-threshold and confidential nature
of DMHIs may appeal especially to young people with SAD [30,72]. Thus, this systematic review
and meta-analysis aims to evaluate the efficacy of DMHI in reducing social anxiety symptoms in
children, adolescents and young adults. 
Our primary research question is: Are DMHIs efficacious in reducing social anxiety symptoms in
children, adolescents and young adults? Secondary research questions include moderator analyses: Is
the between-group effect size of DMHIs moderated by control condition, age, the underlying
psychological principle of the DMHI (i.e., CBT, other interventions), the intervention focus (SAD-
specific or anxiety disorders in general), or human support (i.e., guided self-help vs. unguided self-

Method
The study protocol for this review and meta-analysis was published on the International Prospective
Register of Systematic Reviews PROSPERO, ID: CRD42023424181, on 15.08.2023 and conducted
following the PRISMA guidelines. 
Search Strategy and Study Selection
Search Strategy 
Six electronic databases (APA PsycINFO, Embase, MEDLINE, PsyIndex, PubMed, Web of Science)
were searched on 10 July 2024. The search was restricted to journal articles in the PsycInfo and
Medline databases and randomised controlled trials in the Embase, Medline and Pubmed databases.
Details  for  the  full  search  string  and  all  syntaxes  are  given  in  the  multimedia  appendix  1.
Furthermore, the clinical trials database ClinicalTrials.gov was searched on 31 July 2024 for studies
not identified in the electronic search, which yielded no additional studies. Previously published
meta-analyses on DMHIs for anxiety disorders in children and adolescents were checked for studies
not found with the database search. 
Eligibility Criteria
Studies needed to meet the following inclusion criteria: 1) including young people with a mean age
between 0 to <25 years [73,74], 2) evaluating a DMHI based on psychological principles that can be
used remotely, 3) in a randomised controlled trial (RCT) comparing an experimental group to
another active intervention, a waitlist, care-as-usual or treatment-as-usual condition, 4) reporting an
outcome measure assessing social anxiety and 5) is published in a peer-reviewed journal in English
or German. Study protocols, reviews, or meta-analyses were excluded. 
The age range of under 25 years was selected to reflect current theories in child and adolescent
psychiatry [75,76] and epidemiological evidence indicating the age of onset of SAD often occurs in
this age range  [6]. The rationale for including both prevention and treatment in the diagnostic
spectrum is based on the understanding that social anxiety symptoms exist on a continuum, and the
intervention  techniques  underlying  both  the  prevention  and  treatment  of  SAD  are  similar
[12,13,21,77–79]. 
Study Selection 
Titles, abstracts and full texts were systematically reviewed. Author NW extracted all relevant
information (title, abstract) for the study selection process to Excel. Independently, authors NW and
AF first screened all titles and second all abstracts of the records identified through the searches. No
specialised software was used. In case of discrepancies between the raters, the records were moved
to the next screening round. Afterwards, NW and AF reviewed the full texts of the remaining
records. If they disagreed about inclusion or exclusion, they discussed each study decision until
consensus. If they did not reach an agreement, the final decision was taken together with SJS. Since
the consensus method was followed, no inter-rater reliability was calculated. 
Data Extraction
The data were extracted by two authors (NW and AF) independently into an Excel file as prepared in
advance following the Cochrane Data Collection Form (Higgins et al., 2020). No automation tools
were used. Information extracted from primary studies included study characteristics (i.e., country,
ethical  approval,  trial  preregistration,  funding,  study  aims,  study  design,  recruitment  method,
randomisation procedure, justification of sample size, dropouts, handling of missing values), sample

Data Analysis
Data analyses followed the practical guide for meta-analyses in mental health research [80] and were
analysed with the Comprehensive Meta-Analysis (CMA) version 4.0 software [81]. For each study, a
standardised effect size was computed using Hedges’ g and the 95% confidence intervals derived
from means, standard deviations and sample sizes of the intervention and control groups. To account
for potential heterogeneity, all analyses were computed with random effects models [80]. In 3-armed
RCTs, we prioritised comparisons with the waitlist control group over another active control group
as there were more studies with passive comparison conditions leading to a more homogenous
comparison group [e.g., 82–85]. 
Heterogeneity was assessed through visual inspection of the forest plot, the Q-test and the calculation
of the I2 statistic. The Q-test assesses whether the observed effect sizes significantly differ from each
other beyond chance. A significant Q-test indicates heterogeneity, but in small meta-analyses, there
might be insufficient power to detect it [80]. I2 values explain the proportion of heterogeneity, that is,
the variability in treatment effect estimates due to real study differences and not due to chance, with
25% indicating low, 50% moderate and 75% high heterogeneity [86]. Additionally, we calculated Tau
and Tau2. Tau measures the standard deviation of the true effects across studies, while Tau2 is its
square representing the estimated between-study variance. A significant Tau2 suggests heterogeneity,
supporting the use of random effects models [87]. 
As high heterogeneity was expected, we explored possible sources of heterogeneity in moderator
analyses. Moderators were explored in subgroup analyses if the moderator was categorical (i.e.,
control condition, Risk of Bias, psychological principles, SAD-specific intervention, guidance,
parental involvement, age group) or in a meta-regression analysis if the moderator was continuous
(i.e., age, symptom severity). Subgroup analyses included the computation of average effect sizes
and the Q-statistic as an indicator of a common effect size. A significant Q-statistic suggests
heterogeneity in effect sizes within the subgroup. 
Sensitivity analyses were performed to investigate the robustness of our findings. We conducted
analyses excluding studies with the highest and lowest effect sizes to assess the potential influence of
outliers on the meta-analytic results. This allowed us to evaluate the stability of the overall effect size
when extreme values were omitted. In another sensitivity analysis, we only included studies with
participants who all met diagnostic criteria for SAD to control for heterogeneous diagnostic samples.
Moreover, in a subgroup analysis, studies were stratified based on their Risk of Bias ratings to
investigate the influence of the methodological quality. In addition to these preregistered analyses,
we conducted a sensitivity analysis by excluding studies with an age range over 25 years to test
whether older participants altered the effects and we carried out a moderator analysis with age groups
(children (>10 years), adolescents (11-17 years), young adults (<18 years)) as moderator. 
Additionally, numbers needed to treat (NNT) were computed for all effect sizes using the dmetar
package in R with the default Kraemer and Kupfer method  [88]. NNT indicate the number of
participants to be treated to achieve one case with reliable symptom change. 
To address a possible publication bias, we visually inspected the funnel plot for symmetry and

Risk of Bias Assessment
Risk of bias was assessed by two reviewers (NW, AF) independently following the Risk of Bias
Assessment (RoB2) procedure and using the RoB2 Excel template [92]. After the individual rating,
discrepancies between ratings were discussed until consensus was reached. The RoB2 tool includes
five domains for randomised trials: 1) Bias arising from the randomisation process, 2) bias due to
deviations  from  intended  interventions,  3)  bias  due  to  missing  outcome  data,  4)  bias  in  the
measurement of the outcome and 5) bias in the selection of the reported result. Risk of bias was rated
separately for each domain based on whether it was likely to have influenced study outcomes.
Studies categorised as low overall risk have low bias ratings in all five domains. Some concerns in at
least one domain led to some concerns about the overall judgment of the study. High Risk of Bias
was assigned to studies with at least one domain rated as high risk [80].

removed before screening as they were duplicates over different databases. After the title screening,
1357 records, and after the abstract screening, 205 records, respectively, were excluded due to
nonfulfillment of eligibility criteria. Therefore, 113 full texts were assessed for eligibility. Finally, 22
records were included in the systematic review and 21 in the meta-analysis. The study by Vigerland
and colleagues [93] was not included in the meta-analytic calculations because there was insufficient
data of social anxiety at post-assessment. For a complete overview, consider the flow diagram in

Study Characteristics
The selected 22 papers were published between 2011 and 2024. The 22 trials were conducted in
Australia (k = 7), USA (k=4), Sweden (k=3), UK (k=2), China (k=2), Spain (k=1), Iran (k=1),
Canada (k=1) and the Netherlands (k=1). For a detailed overview of study characteristics, see Table

Sample Characteristics
Participants  were  recruited  via  different  strategies,  mostly  sought  in  self-selected  sampling
procedures  for  instance  through  posters  and  flyers,  word  of  mouth,  social  media  and  online

mental health clinic [109]. Few studies mentioned efforts to ensure a broad socioeconomic range
either by choosing schools that cover multiple socioeconomic backgrounds [108] or by aligning the
demographic profile of the study cohort to the country [56].

Intervention Characteristics
For a detailed overview of intervention characteristics, consider Table 2. All interventions
were  delivered  via  computer,  except  two  studies  that  investigated  a  smartphone
application (app) [96,105]. In most studies, participants completed the interventions from
home (or from anywhere) and only some interventions were delivered at a lab [82] or in a
school setting [83] under the observation of a researcher or teacher, respectively. Except
for the apps and the social skills game, all interventions were module-based. Participants
either had access to all modules from the beginning or one module was activated per
week. 
Most interventions were based on CBT principles (k=16); some were CBM interventions
(k=3) and some were based on mindfulness (k=1) or social skills training (k=1). The
interventions  either  addressed  anxiety  symptoms  or  disorders  in  general  or  were
specifically
 
designed
 
for
 
SAD.
 
SAD-specific
 
interventions
[56,85,98,101,103,104,106,108,110],  particularly  incorporated  examples  of  fears  and
cognitions that are important in SAD (for example based on the Cognitive Model [12] or
the cognitive-behavioral model  [14])or focused on different biases that play a role in
SAD [82,84,105]. 
Adherence
Most commonly, adherence was operationalised as a mean number of completed modules
per  participant  or  percentage  of  participants  that  completed  individual  modules
[56,82,84,93,94,100–102,104–107]. Leigh and Clark  [108] reported the average time
spent  with  the  intervention  and  Nordh  and  colleagues  [98] applied  the  “Internet
Intervention Patient Adherence Scale” [111]. Only one study [100] analysed the effect of
adherence on symptom reduction and found a dose effect with more completed modules
resulting in a greater change from baseline to post treatment. 
Guidance
The extent of guidance varied greatly over all studies. Nine studies were unguided self-
help; in six studies participants had no contact [94,96,99,104,105,107] and three studies
sent automated reminders or summary emails [84,97,103]. In two studies the presence of
teachers or researchers ensured that participants were using the DMHI but they gave no
feedback on the intervention progress [82,83]. Most programs individualised their human
guidance in asynchronous weekly messages in the program, via email or as feedback on
worksheets [56,85,93,98,100,101,108]. Some offered scheduled or on-request telephone
support during the DMHI time [97,98,100,102,106,108,109]. One study offered weekly
in-person meetings with a trained undergraduate student in the role of a peer coach [110].
Generally, guidance was provided by trained Psychology graduate students under regular
supervision  or  by  trained  (CBT-)therapists.  Some  studies  indicated  time  spent  for
guidance, e.g., [108] reported an overall mean of 398.67 minutes (6.64 h) spent directly
communicating with participants. Other studies recorded the average effort per week and
participant (15 to 30 minutes;[85,98]) or the average meeting time (5 to 35 minutes;
[97,106,110]).

Risk of Bias 
Risk of Bias assessment indicated that most studies showed some concerns (see Figure
2). A main concern was the fact that RCTs investigating psychological intervention do not
facilitate adequate masking of participants to the treatment condition. Therefore, only
studies with another active intervention that mimicked the initial intervention and in
which participants were blinded to the condition (e.g., [98]) were rated with low risk in
the dimension 4 Measurement of the outcome. Three studies were classified as high risk
due to insufficient information on randomisation, no indication of analysis to estimate the
effect of assignment to the intervention group, high missingness and no indication of any
measures that account for that [85,94,99]. Insufficient information on the randomisation
process (i.e., concealment of allocation sequence or assessment of baseline differences
between groups) was reported in 39% (9/22) of the studies. In 30% (7/22) of the studies,
there was  no information  on possible trial  deviation  or estimation  of an effect of
assignment to the intervention. Most studies (83%; 19/22) followed the intention-to-treat
(ITT) approach and investigated their missing data. All but five studies (22%) reported
their plan of analysis and the reporting of the result was transparent and complete. The
Risk of Bias assessments of all studies are included in the supplementary material.

Publication Bias
Visual inspection of the funnel plot suggested the presence of a publication bias as the
standard errors were not equally scattered around the overall mean (see Figure 4). Both
the Begg and Mazumdar’s test and the Egger’s test of the intercept were significant (both
p>.001), supporting an underlying publication bias. When adjusting for missing studies
using the Duval and Tweedie trim and fill procedure in the random effects model, the
effect size resulted in g=0.506 (95% CI= 0.308 to 0.707).

Discussion
This meta-analysis evaluated the efficacy of DMHIs on SAD in young people. Across 21
RCTs and 4196 participants, we found a small to medium pooled effect size in favour of
DMHIs  compared to both inactive and active control conditions  assessed after the
intervention. The effect size remained after taking the publication bias into account.
Notably, the treatment gains decreased until 3 to 6 months follow-up and were no longer
significant at 12 months follow-up. Subgroup analyses revealed that DMHIs based on
CBT, including SAD-specific elements and support by human guidance carried out in
adolescents and young adults resulted in higher pooled effect sizes compared to other
psychological interventions, transdiagnostic interventions for several anxiety disorders
and unguided self-help programmes in children. Mean age and parental involvement had
no significant effect on the outcome.

Compared to other meta-analyses of DMHIs addressing anxiety disorders, the overall
effect size found in this meta-analysis is smaller. In a sample with the same age span but
mixed anxiety disorders, a meta-analysis found a large pooled effect size (g=0.68) [45].
Even in children and adolescents at risk for an anxiety disorder (i.e., subthreshold of
diagnosis), where smaller effect sizes would be expected, a large pooled effect size
(SMD=0.77)  was  found  [44].  Possible  reasons  for  the  smaller  effect  compared  to
previous meta-analyses could be the large heterogeneity between the included studies
regarding the interventions, the samples and the study designs. Another reason could be
the fact that despite the focus of this meta-analysis on SAD samples the primary studies
included a broad range of mental health problems or disorders (e.g., other anxiety
disorders, depression). Consequently, study participants did not necessarily suffer from
social  anxiety.  In  other  meta-analyses,  either  a  measure  assessing  various  anxiety
disorders was defined as the primary outcome, or the interventions and samples were
primarily geared towards SAD. The sensitivity analysis in this meta-analysis showed that
when only studies with participants with a SAD diagnosis were included, the calculated
effect  size  was  large  (g=1.15).  Meta-analytic  evidence  of  face-to-face  settings  for
children and adolescents diagnosed with SAD only also yielded a larger effect (g= 0.71)
that even increased until follow-up [22]. Similar effect sizes [112] were found in ICBTs
for adults with SAD (g=0.55[112];  g=0.76 [113]). In line with this, the moderator
analyses showed that there are, in some cases, indeed large differences between the effect
sizes of the subgroups. These differences are discussed in the following. 
Psychological Principles of Effective DMHIs
The  superiority  of  CBT-based  treatments  over  other  treatments,  such  as  ICBM  or
mindfulness  exercises  is  also  reflected  in  other  studies  [58,114].  However,  most
treatments for young people with SAD are based on CBT and the evidence for other
treatments is still limited  [115]. Interestingly, SAD-specific treatments, including CBT
and  CBM,  outperformed  treatments  addressing  anxiety  in  general.  This  finding
contradicts RCTs directly comparing two treatments (transdiagnostic anxiety vs. SAD-
specific, i.e.,[55,56]) but aligns with other evidence suggesting adolescents with SAD
have poorer chances compared to other anxiety disorders  [53,54,116]. This finding is
quite striking because, DMHIs addressing SAD in adults have an extensive evidence base
with  RCTs  demonstrating  efficacious  interventions  based  on  CBT,  psychodynamic
therapy, and acceptance-based interventions [39] and meta-analytic evidence supporting
ICBT to be equally efficacious compared to face-to-face CBT [42]. In young people, the
question remains as to which treatment components have a generic effect in anxiety
disorders and which would improve the treatment effect in SAD and thus indicate a
disorder-specific treatment. Treatment components specific to SAD are theoretically
discussed in aetiological and maintenance models of SAD  [12–14]. So far, there is
limited empirical evidence of whether these components are specifically important for
young people with SAD [21]. It would be valuable to test both transdiagnostic anxiety
and SAD-specific components individually in interventions. Compared to RCTs so far,
study designs such as factorial or leapfrog designs would be more suitable for obtaining
information on the efficacy of individual components and the optimal compilation of
those components to an intervention [117–120]. 
Supporting Factors in DMHI Implementation for Young People

Similar  to previous  evidence,  moderator  analyses  supported  the  efficacy  of  guided
DMHIs but not unguided DMHIs [59]. Notably, there was great variety in the extent of
human guidance: Some studies provided participants only with summaries of exercises
via chat or email, while others phoned them regularly. The amount of support was
quantified only in a minority of the trials. Furthermore, while some guidance formats
reflected strategies to improve engagement with the intervention, the guidance of other
trials could be viewed as an additional treatment in a format of blended therapy. To
investigate how to optimise the amount, the content or the delivery setting of guidance for
young people, future papers should be more detailed in their rapport of the guidance
format and purpose and may even compare different ways of supporting young people in
their studies [e.g., 121]. Possible results could streamline future implementation research
and support the use of and continuous engagement with digital approaches. 
Another aspect that varied considerably across studies was parental involvement. In one
study, the intervention was only directed at parents [97], while others included specific
parent information or modules that complemented the children’s modules [56,93,98,109],
or only addressed the young people. Research to date shows an inconclusive picture of
how parents can be best involved in treatments to increase their overall effectiveness
compared to child/adolescent only interventions. [122]. Comparable to our results, other
meta-analyses  also  found  no  additional  effect  of  parental  involvement  [123–125].
Especially in adolescence and young adulthood, the social context relevant to anxiety-
inducing situations lies primarily outside the parental home (e.g., at school, with peers).
Therefore, the involvement of parents might be less relevant for symptom reduction.
Nevertheless, for young people parents play an important role in accessing mental health
services [126,127] and could therefore be a crucial supporting factor in their children’s
adherence to the intervention [128].
Adherence has been defined and reported, if at all, in great variety in the included studies.
Most commonly, completed modules or time spent in the DMHI were reported, but
rarely, their effect on treatment outcome. Although these indices give some information
on DMHI usage, they do not provide any information about reasons for non-adherence.
Previously, some efforts have been made to find explanations for DMHI or treatment
non-usage. One explanation given in adult samples is the  good enough effect; that is,
researchers explained the discontinuation of the DMHI in that the participants had
reached their set goals  [129]. This type of participant was also found in a qualitative
examination  of  adolescents  who  stopped  treatment  for  depression.  Thereby,  two
additional  types  were  also  identified:  the  dissatisfied,  those  who  did  not  find  the
intervention helpful and the troubled, those who reported a lack of stability in their lives
to  engage  in  an  intervention  [130].  Future  research  should  report  a  minimum  of
adherence parameters (e.g., completed modules, time spent in the programme) and might
even add reasons for non-adherence. Furthermore, it would be important to report the
analysis of whether adherence influenced the outcome within the publication reporting
the main results of the RCT. With this information, studies could be better compared and
the influence of adherence on the outcome could be assessed meta-analytically [131]. 
Moderators of Treatment Efficacy
Regarding potential moderators, age did not emerge as a moderator of treatment efficacy
in the meta-regression analysis, but subgroup analysis reported significantly higher effect
sizes for adolescents and young adults compared to children. Likewise, other meta-

analyses have indicated that older adolescents profit more from DMHIs than children

primary studies included wide age ranges and did not report results for different age
groups. This highlights the need to replicate the results in future studies. 
Symptom severity at the beginning of the intervention moderated treatment outcome with
a small effect. In other studies, larger treatment effects were also found for adults with
higher symptom severity  [132]. The evidence in childhood and adolescence is mixed;
some meta-analyses or studies found a significant effect while others did not [133]. This
result, therefore, is in line with the overall mixed picture. 
Designing and implementing DMHIs in the Future
This review included DMHIs that were developed primarily in high-income countries and
provided in English, Swedish, Chinese, Persian, Spanish, or Dutch. Furthermore, few
undertook efforts to match the socio-economic composition of the study with that of the
home country [56,108]. A recurring argument advancing DMHIs is that they offer help at
low cost, low threshold and high accessibility to young people from non-urban and lower
financial  sectors  [38,134,135].  However,  this  presumption  can  only  be  realised  if
measures are taken to design DMHIs for diverse target groups, evaluate them in different
demographic groups and support their implementation, particularly in those groups that
otherwise have little access to mental health care. To achieve this, young people need to
be  actively  involved  in  designing  interventions  and  validation  studies,  recruiting
participants  for  trials  and  communicating  about  and  implementing  digital  self-help
options [115,136]. 
Limitations and Implications for Future Research
This review and meta-analysis have some limitations and the results should be considered
thoughtfully. Firstly, the meta-analysis and included subgroup analyses are based on a
small number of studies and results should be interpreted cautiously. The limited number
of studies precluded an examination of the efficacy of specific intervention components
(e.g., psychoeducation, exposure therapy, social skills training, CBM or relaxation and
mindfulness exercises) or for diagnostic categories (subclinical vs. clinical). Most studies
included participants with elevated scores in social anxiety screenings based on self-
reports and few of the identified prevention studies made diagnostic efforts to distinguish
youths with subclinical social anxiety symptoms from those with a previous or current
SAD diagnosis. Thus, prevention and treatment studies could not be categorised reliably
to investigate potential differential treatment effects in subgroup analyses. Moreover, only
two studies provided long-term follow-up data, resulting in limited information on the
sustainability of treatment effects. Next, this meta-analysis encompassed a broad age
range, spanning distinct developmental stages and diverse target groups. For instance,
while adolescents are typically the primary end-users of digital interventions, in the case
of preschool-aged children parents or legal guardians generally serve as the end-users,
given that young children often lack the cognitive and emotional capacities to engage
with such interventions independently. To explore potential age-related effects, subgroup
analyses were conducted across three age categories (i.e., children, adolescents, young

adults). However, these analyses were limited by the small number of primary studies
within each subgroup and did not account for developmental differences  within or

these broad categories, future research should examine potential moderator effects using
more granular  age  distinctions  and developmental profiles, particularly  in  terms  of
cognitive, emotional, and social competencies. 
Furthermore, factors  influencing the efficacy, such as  adherence, were inadequately
reported in primary studies, preventing their incorporation into the meta-analysis, for
instance, in a meta-regression. Lastly, the methodological quality of the studies varied in
terms  of  transparency  in  randomisation,  masking  procedures  and  consideration  of

Future intervention studies should, therefore, be methodologically more rigorous, include
thorough diagnostic procedures, provide long-term follow-up data and report on potential
moderators  such  as  adherence,  parental  involvement,  guidance  and  participant
characteristics in more detail. Moreover, more details on how access to the modules was
managed  would be interesting as  it  may  have  an  impact  on the adherence  of the
participants as well as on the efficacy of the intervention. Furthermore, future studies
should  aim  to  target  a  socioeconomically  diverse  study  population  and  report  on
demographic information that may influence the availability and efficacy of treatments
(e.g., gender, age, country, urbanicity, socioeconomic status, culture and minoritised
status,  [137]).  This  additional  information  could  inform  future  meta-analyses  or,
preferably, meta-analyses of individual participant data that don’t rely on summary
statistics to investigate subgroup effects. 
Conclusions
In conclusion, while further research is necessary to provide conclusive evidence on the
efficacy of DMHIs for young people, our meta-analysis and review shows their potential
to alleviate SAD. The preliminary findings underscore the importance of future research
efforts  aimed  at  clarifying  the  specific  treatment  components  that  render  DMHIs
effective,  optimising  human  guidance  strategies  and  individualising  interventions  to
enhance therapeutic outcomes. Moreover, our study highlights inclusivity in DMHIs and
research design, emphasising the need for future studies to encompass participants from
