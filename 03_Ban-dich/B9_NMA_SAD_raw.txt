School of Education Science, Jiangsu Second Normal University, Nanjing, China

A B S T R A C T
Background: Social anxiety disorder (SAD) is a high-prevalence mental disorder among children and adolescents. 
The aim of this study is to compare and rank the effectiveness of several psychotherapies for SAD among children 
and adolescents.
Methods: Only randomized controlled trials (RCTs) were utilized by searching PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Li­
brary, and Web of Science. We used network meta-analysis in the Bayesian framework to analyze the data. This 
study is registered with PROSPERO, number CRD42023476829.
Results: In total, 30 RCTs with 1547 individuals were included, and nine psychotherapies with three control 
conditions were compared and ranked in this study. The findings revealed that internet-delivered cognitive 
behavioural therapy (surface under the cumulative ranking curve [SUCRA: 71.2 %]), group cognitive behav­
ioural therapy (SUCRA: 68.4 %), and individual cognitive behavioural therapy (SUCRA: 66.0 %) significantly 
reduced social anxiety symptoms; internet-delivered cognitive behavioural therapy also significantly decreased 
depression symptoms in these patients (SUCRA: 92.2 %). In addition, group cognitive behavioural therapy can 
enhance functioning in these patients (SUCRA: 89.6 %).
Conclusion: These results suggest that internet-delivered cognitive behavioural therapy is the optimal type of 
psychotherapy for reducing social anxiety and depression symptoms in children and adolescents with SAD, 
internet-delivered parent-child interaction therapy and cognitive bias modification of interpretation have rela­
tively poor treatment effects on social anxiety symptoms in children than other psychological interventions, and 
group cognitive behavioural therapy has better benefits in enhancing the functioning among children and ad­
olescents with SAD. Further studies are needed to ascertain these results due to the limited number of included 
studies.
1. Introduction
Social anxiety disorder (SAD) is a common mental disorder among 
children and adolescents, with a lifetime prevalence of approximately 
10 % (Burstein et al., 2011; Davidson et al., 1994). The average age at 
which children are diagnosed with SAD is approximately 9.2 years 
(Burstein et al., 2011). This condition is often comorbid with depressive 
disorder (Stein et al., 2001). Moreover, several functional impairments 
(including poor academic achievement, interpersonal relationship is­
sues, and dropping out of school) were reported (Beidel et al., 2007; 
Rapee and Spence, 2004). Although the high prevalence of SAD, few 
younger people with SAD receive effective treatments (Merikangas 
et al., 2011; Wittchen and Fehm, 2003). That could be because SAD 
symptoms are frequently mistaken for shyness and that it would fade 
with age (Dalrymple and Zimmerman, 2011). However, SAD tends to 
persist without effective treatment (Beidel et al., 1996; Bruce et al., 
2005). Therefore, early intervention is necessary for youth with SAD.
With the focus on youth with SAD, a range of effective psychological 
intervention programmes have emerged. Social Effectiveness Training 
for Children and Adolescents (SET–C) is a behavioural therapy (BT) 
that consist of education, social skills training, peer generalization, and 
exposure. SET-C is delivered through group training and individual 
exposure, and research findings have shown that SET-C can significantly 
reduce social anxiety among youth (Beidel et al., 2000). Later re­
searchers developed a school-based group cognitive behavioural ther­
apy (CBT), Skills for Academic and Social Success (SASS), which 
includes education, social skills training, exposure, realistic thinking, 
and relapse prevention. Of these, SET-C is largely used to inform the

cents (CBGT-A) is a group cognitive behavioural therapy that includes 
education, social skills, problem-solving skills, assertiveness, cognitive 
restructuring, and exposure (Albano et al., 1995). Research finding has 
shown that the therapy reduces social anxiety compared to control 
group (Hayward et al., 2000). Intervention in Adolescents with Gener­
alized Social Phobia (IAFS) is another effective group cognitive behav­
ioural therapy that has been applied in the spanish adolescents, where 
this group treatment consists of education, training in social skills, 
exposure, and cognitive restructuring (Olivares et al., 2002). It can be 
suggested that BT and CBT for treating SAD in youth commonly include 
components such as education, social skills training, and exposure 
(Masia Warner et al., 2007); the difference between the two lies in the 
presence or absence of the cognitive restructuring (Yang et al., 2019; 
Zhou et al., 2019). CBT is a widely used psychological intervention to 
treat social anxiety that specifically emphasises cognition is crucial to 
maintain SAD (Pilling et al., 2013; Rapee and Heimberg, 1997; Rapee 
et al., 2009; Wong and Rapee, 2016), and therefore focuses on cognitive 
restructuring which encourage children to recognize and modify irra­
tional and automatic beliefs associated with social interactions (Iverach 
and Rapee, 2014; O'Toole et al., 2015). To enhance the therapeutic ef­
fect, researchers have developed a new CBT program for treating SAD 
among youth based on the model of Clark and Well (1995), which em­
phasises intervening in individuals' overly negative beliefs about social 
interactions through attention training, updating negative impressions 
with the help of video feedback, and behavioural experiments (Leigh 
and Clark, 2018; Spence and Rapee, 2016). A review has been shown 
that the results of psychological interventions based on the model of 
Clark and Well (1995) in adolescents are encouraging (Leigh and Clark, 
2018). CBT is typically delivered by face-to-face format (Mancini et al., 
2005). Due to the stigmatization of psychotherapy, the high cost of 
treatment, and the core symptoms of SAD, receiving face-to-face 
psychotherapy is quite hard for those younger patients (Ollendick and 
Hirshfeld-Becker, 2002; Radez et al., 2021). With the rapid development 
of digital technology in the psychological interventions and the highly 
structured nature of CBT, a range of research findings suggest that 
internet-delivered CBT is also effective in reducing social anxiety in 
adolescents with SAD (Erwin et al., 2004; Ollendick and Hirshfeld- 
Becker, 2002; Ollendick et al., 2018). Psychodynamic therapy (PDT) 
suggests that social anxiety disorder is associated with an individual's 
underlying core conflictual relationship theme (Leichsenring et al., 
2015); therapists can help children reduce their social anxiety symptoms 
by integrating supportive and expressive interventions (Salzer et al., 
2018). Most of the recent psychological interventions for SAD are aimed 
at childhood and adolescence (ages 7+), but there are fewer in­
terventions for early children. One study used internet-delivered parent- 
child interaction therapy (Int-PCIT) to treat early children (3-8 years) 
with SAD, which remodels child behaviour by directly reshaping the 
parent-child interaction; the result showed a positive intervention effect 
on SAD (Comer et al., 2021). In addition, there have been studies using 
computer-based treatment, for example, attention bias modification 
treatment (ABMT) focuses on decreasing attentional bias by shifting 
children's attention away from threat stimuli to reduce through using 
the dot-probe task, which in turn reduces social anxiety symptoms in 
youth (Ollendick et al., 2019). However, which psychotherapeutic 
approach, form of intervention (individual, group, or a combination of 
both), and mode of delivery (face-to-face or internet) is more beneficial 
for treating children and adolescents with SAD is still under discussion. 
Previous meta-analyses showed that specific types of psychotherapy are 
effective for youth with SAD (Scaini et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2019). 
However, these meta-analyses only compared one or two psychological 
interventions for youth with SAD to a control condition. In addition, 
traditional meta-analysis can only analyze direct comparison studies. 
Therefore, the optimal type of psychotherapy for the children and 
adolescent with SAD remains undetermined.

2. Methods
2.1. Literature search
We searched PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, and Web of Sci­
ence from the time of their inception to 25 August 2023. This study was 
performed using the PRISMA instruction manual (Hutton et al., 2015). 
The PICOS tool served as a framework for the search approach. Ap­
pendix Table S1 presents more detailed information regarding the 
literature search. Our protocol was registered with PROSPERO, number 
CRD42023476829.
The inclusion criteria were applied: (1) individuals under the age of 
18 with a clinical diagnosis of SAD were eligible for enrolment; (2) 
experimental group with different types of psychological intervention; 
(3) control condition including waitlist, no treatment, and psychological 
placebo; (4) randomized controlled trials; and (5) social anxiety symp­
toms, depression symptoms, and functioning were measured as out­
comes by using psychological scales (Appendix Table S2).
2.2. Literature screening and data extraction
We use Endnote X9 to manage literature search records. The titles in 
the literature were independently screened by two researchers. There­
after, abstracts were reviewed, the full articles were examined, and their 
inclusion or exclusion was determined. If two researchers disagreed on a 
particular study, a third researcher decided on whether it should be 
included.
The following preselected approach and standards were used to 
extract data: study name, country, definition of SAD, age, sample, 
intervention, control, and outcome.
2.3. Quality assessment
According to the Cochrane Handbook version 5.1, two separate 
authors assessed the quality of studies. Review Manager (Version 5.4) 
was applied to assess the risk of bias (ROB). Seven indicators were used 
to assess ROB: random sequence generation, allocation concealment, 
blinding of participants and personnel, blinding of outcome assessment, 
incomplete outcome data, selective reporting, and other biases (Higgins 
et al., 2011). Trials were classified as high risk (three or more items 
assessed as high risk of bias), low risk (five or more items rated as low 
risk of bias and no more than one as high risk of bias), or moderate risk 
(all remaining trials) (Yang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2019).
2.4. Data analysis
The variable in the analysis was continuous and reported as mean 
with standard deviation. The random-effects model was used for anal­
ysis as possible distinctions between studies are unavoidable (Jackson 
et al., 2011). The Bayesian framework-based network meta-analysis was 
applied by using Stata software (version 15). Using the Markov chains 
Monte Carlo approach, we generated mean differences (MD) with 95 % 
confidence intervals (CI) (Vats et al., 2019). The nodal splitting 
approach was used with Stata software instructions to quantify and 
demonstrate agreement between indirect and direct comparisons. The 
thickness of the line indicates the number of studies between in­
terventions, while the size of the nodes indicates the sample size 
(Chaimani et al., 2013). The consistency was confirmed if P > 0.05.
The effect size of interventions was ranked using the SUCRA. In the 
SUCRA, the intervention with the highest probability of being the best is 
indicated by a bigger area under the curve (Salanti et al., 2011). 
Furthermore, by generating a funnel plot and employing the symmetry 
criterion for a visual inspection, publication bias in the network meta- 
analysis was verified.
3. Results
In total, 4058 articles were found through the literature search. After 
eliminating duplicates, the remaining 3457 articles were read for titles 
and abstracts, and 3336 articles were excluded. Following the full-text 
review of 121 articles, 91 were excluded for various reasons, including 
the inclusion of adults and non-social anxiety disorder patients, the non- 
randomized design, the lack of a standardized diagnosis of SAD, and lack 
of available data. Finally, our network meta-analysis included 30 
studies. Fig. 1 depicts the screening procedure in detail.
This network meta-analysis includes 30 RCTs with 1547 participants.

4. Discussion
To our knowledge, this is the first network meta-analysis that in­
cludes randomized controlled trials on several different psychological 
treatments for children and adolescents with SAD. Our results suggest 
that internet-delivered CBT is the optimal type of psychological inter­
vention for reducing social anxiety and depression symptoms of children 
and adolescents with SAD. Group CBT has a greater impact on enhancing 
the functioning in these individuals than other psychological 
interventions.
Our results revealed that compared with the waitlist group, internet- 
delivered CBT, group CBT, and individual CBT significantly reduced 
social anxiety symptoms. Similar to the results of previous studies (Ingul 
et al., 2014; Nordh et al., 2021; Olivares et al., 2014), multiple forms of 
CBT which consist common therapeutic components (social skill 
training, exposure, and cognitive restructuring) have positive thera­
peutic effects on SAD among youth. Most of the participants in the 
current relevant studies were over seven years old, which shows that 
CBT can have positive intervention effects on SAD at childhood and 
adolescence. Due to the prevalence of SAD increases from childhood to 
adolescence (Halldorsson and Creswell, 2017; Spence and Rapee, 2016), 
our results may imply that future studies could consider to conduct CBT 
in youth as early as childhood (ages 7+). Moreover, compared with 
other psychological interventions, internet-delivered CBT may be the 
optimal type of psychotherapy for SAD in children and adolescents. 
First, in terms of treatment content, internet-delivered CBT emphasize 
reversing the specific cognition and behaviour that persist and develop 
social anxiety which is based on the model of Clark and Wells (1995). 
Social anxiety is driven by overly negative beliefs about social in­
teractions that are maintained by self-focus, dysfunctional safety be­
haviours, and negative self-image (Leigh and Clark, 2018; Spence et al., 
2017). Therefore, the treatment emphasises specific processes of atten­
tion training, video feedback, and behavioural experiment. These factors 
may contribute to the significant therapeutic effects of internet- 
delivered CBT. Second, in terms of treatment participants, lower likeli­
hood of seeking traditional delivered treatment for youth than adults 
(Radez et al., 2021). Given the high level of digital engagement in daily 
lives for children (Hollis et al., 2020) and the core symptoms of SAD, 
internet-delivered intervention may be more appealing to them (Leigh 
and Clark, 2018; Leigh et al., 2023; Olfson et al., 2000). Third, in terms 
of treatment form, internet-delivered CBT has unique advantages in 
terms of low cost of treatment, no need for long-distance care, and no 
restrictions on the location of the treatment. Moreover, CBT is fit for 
internet delivery because it is highly structured and usually consists of 
clear therapy components (Ollendick et al., 2018). Therefore, internet- 
delivered CBT may be the optional form of psychological intervention 
among children and adolescents with SAD. In addition, our network 
meta-analysis results showed that internet-delivered parent-child 
interaction therapy and cognitive bias modification of interpretation 
have relatively poor treatment effects on social anxiety symptoms in 
children compared to other psychological interventions. First, only one 
study each using internet-delivered parent-child interaction therapy and 
cognitive bias modification of interpretation among the studies included 
in this analysis. Second, the internet-delivered parent-child interaction 
therapy intervention was the only study that targeted children 3–8 years 
old with SAD; given the limited cognition of the young children, the 
intervention was not directed at the children themselves, but in the 
parent and child interaction, with a therapist using a webcam to instruct 
parents how to help their children gradually cope with the different 
levels of exposure (Comer et al., 2021). These factors may lead to 
treatment effects that are not entirely obvious in our network meta- 
analysis results. Cognitive bias modification of interpretation is 
another computer-based treatment. Considering the children's motiva­
tion to participate, the researchers adapted the experimental procedure 
and intensity for adults; however, it does not seem to have a good effect 
on SAD in children (Orchard et al., 2017).

Our findings reveal that internet-delivered CBT could also reduce 
depression symptoms in children and adolescents who suffer from SAD 
than the waitlist group, that is similar with previous study (Leigh and 
Clark, 2023). This may be because depression involves maladaptive 
cognitive processes similar to those in social anxiety, such as irrational 
perceptions of reality, excessive self-focus, safety behaviours, and 
avoidance behaviours (Rapee and Spence, 2004). Social anxiety and 
depression in adolescents may have a synergistic effect (Hayward et al., 
2000), and thus the treatment of SAD may have generalized effects, with 
a similar reduction in depression (¨Ost et al., 2015). Our network meta- 
analysis results seem to suggest this as well, with internet-delivered 
CBT significantly decreasing social anxiety and comorbid depression 
symptoms in children and adolescents. Further studies are required on 
the topic.
Our results suggest that group CBT significantly improved func­
tioning in children and adolescents who suffer from SAD compared with 
the waitlist group. According to one review, children with SAD may 
benefit from face-to-face form of cognitive therapies, which can provide 
more opportunities for important exposure experiences like practising 
social skills and receiving immediate feedback on social performance in 
the group (Barkowski et al., 2016). However, due to the small sample 
sizes, further confirmation is required.
Our study has some limitations. Heterogeneity in the original 
research was inevitable (e.g., proportions of studies by gender and re­
gion, different cultures and ethnic minorities). Moreover, our study 
showed limited statistical power for direct head-to-head comparisons 
due to the small sample size for some nodes. Further studies are needed 
to confirm our hypotheses.
5. Conclusions
This study performed a network meta-analysis to compare and rank 
different types of psychotherapies for social anxiety disorder among 
children and adolescents. Our study indicates that internet-delivered 
cognitive behavioural therapy is the optimal type of psychotherapy for 
reducing social anxiety and depression symptoms in children and ado­
lescents with social anxiety disorder. In addition, internet-delivered 
parent-child interaction therapy and cognitive bias modification of 
interpretation have relatively poor treatment effects on social anxiety 
symptoms in children than other psychological interventions. Group 
cognitive behavioural therapy has better benefits in enhancing the 
functioning among children and adolescents with social anxiety 
disorder.
