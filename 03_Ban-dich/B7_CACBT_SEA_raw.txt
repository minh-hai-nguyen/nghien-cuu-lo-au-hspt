A B S T R A C T   
Background: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) has been proven an effective treatment for anxiety disorders. 
However, CBT still dominantly uses concepts and constructs rooted in Western cultures, and most research fo­
cuses on Western populations. It is unsure how this translates to non-Western cultures like Southeast Asia. 
Aims: Our objective is to explore which types of cultural adaptations in CBT have been implemented for anxiety 
disorders in Southeast Asia and their effectiveness. 
Methods: We systematically searched PubMed, PsycINFO, Embase, CENTRAL, GARUDA, and Google Scholar for 
CA-CBT for anxiety disorders in local communities in Southeast Asian countries. Data were analyzed using a 
narrative approach distinguishing between peripheral and core component adaptations. PROSPERO database 
preregistration number was CRD42022336376. 
Results: Seven studies (one randomized controlled trial, three quasi-experimental studies, and three case reports) 
were selected. Two studies made cultural adaptations in multiple components. Two studies modified core 
treatment components by incorporating local values in the CBT restructuring process. Three studies conducted 
cultural adaptation on peripheral treatment components: adaptation to materials and semantics, cultural ex­
amples and themes, and session structure. Three studies did not provide detailed information. One RCT study 
showed better improvement for those who got CA-CBT than those in treatment as usual (TAU). 
Conclusion: The findings suggest some components to consider when conducting cultural adaptation. We could 
not establish the degree of superiority of CA-CBT over non-CA-CBT nor identify components with the most in­
fluence due to the limited number of studies found. Employing standard documentation in reporting trials is also 
important to increase transparency.   
1. Introduction 
Anxiety disorders are among the most prevalent psychiatric disor­
ders, with the highest illness burden and healthcare expenses (Bandelow 
and Michaelis, 2015). The World Health Organization (WHO) estimated 
that 60.05 million (23 % population prevalence) people live with anx­
iety disorders in Southeast Asia (World Health Organization, 2017). In 
Indonesia, around 8 million people (3.3 % of the population) suffer from 
anxiety disorders (World Health Organization, 2017). However, not all 
people with mental illness in Southeast Asia, including Indonesia, get 
professional help due to the limited mental health services and human 
resources (ASEAN Secretariat, 2016). 
The WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) has been 
initiated to scale up services to reduce the mental health gap (World 
Health Organization, 2008). One of the important factors in succeeding 
in scaling up the mental health intervention implementation is cultural 
adaptation (Faregh et al., 2019). Many critics highlight that the concepts 
and interventions developed in Europe or America are out of step with

the diverse cultural values found in non-Western countries (White,

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), one of the most studied psy­
chological treatments worldwide and has been proven to have good 
outcomes across different delivery formats (Baker et al., 2021; Cuijpers 
et al., 2019), dominantly uses concepts and constructs rooted in Western 
cultures (Iwamasa and Hays, 2019; Naeem et al., 2016; Rathod et al., 
2019). This condition may lead to the question of the efficacy of the 
treatment when delivered to the Asian population because of the 
different cultural values, beliefs, and attitudes (Rathod et al., 2018). 
Cultural differences may influence the process of psychosocial in­
terventions (Hwang, 2006; Rathod and Kingdon, 2014). 
To deal with the different cultural gaps, researchers proposed the 
idea of cultural adaptation (Cardemil, 2010; Castro et al., 2010). Cul­
tural adaptation is defined as “the systematic modification of an 
evidence-based treatment (EBT) or intervention protocol to consider 
language, culture, and context in such a way that it is compatible with 
the client’s cultural patterns, meanings, and values” (Bernal et al., 2009, 
p. 362). One way to organize and map adapted components in the cul­
tural adaptation of treatment is by using the classification of the Cultural 
Treatment Adaptation Framework (CTAF) (Chu and Leino, 2017). This 
framework divides adaptation components into two first-order cate­
gories: peripheral and core treatment components. Peripheral treatment 
components focus on making the treatment understandable and 
acceptable, while core treatment components focus on the main treat­
ment components that mediate symptom change. 
According to a review of meta-analyses conducted in 2018, research 
on culturally adapted interventions is still limited, lacks methodological 
rigor, and pays insufficient attention to theoretical foundations and 
cultural considerations. Moreover, most research explored the general 
therapeutic principles, which were not specific to CBT or any other 
specific intervention (Rathod et al., 2018). A previous finding also 
revealed that no clear definition and consistent evidence supports 
implementing any specific type of adaptation to its effectiveness (Healey 
et al., 2017). 
Based on the explanation above, our current research aimed to sys­
tematically review empirical studies of Culturally Adapted CBT (CA- 
CBT), specifically in anxiety disorders focusing on the Southeast Asia 
region. A systematic review with this exclusive scope might help focus 
on revealing the face of cultural adaptation in Southeast Asia, which 
generally has socio-cultural similarities between countries (King, 2008). 
Understanding the cultural adaptation of CBT for anxiety in Southeast 
Asia is expected to gain in-depth information on why and how to 
develop a CBT protocol that will fit local people’s culture and context. In 
summary, this systematic review aimed to answer the following 
questions:  
1. What types of cultural adaptations in CBT have been implemented in 
Southeast Asia for local communities?  
2. What is the effectiveness of CA-CBT in treating anxiety disorders in 
local Southeast Asian populations? 
2. Method 
2.1. Approach and design of the review 
This systematic review followed the guidelines outlined by Preferred 
Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 
(Moher et al., 2009) and was registered on the Prospective Register of 
Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO) (ID: CRD42022336376). For research 
question 1, this systematic review employed studies with various de­
signs (including non-controlled pre-post studies) to thoroughly under­
stand reported cultural adaptations across the literature. For research 
question 2, we only used randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that 
assessed the efficacy of CA-CBT. 
2.2. Inclusion and exclusion criteria 
Inclusion criteria considered (1) target population: Southeast Asian 
people with anxiety disorders under the classification of DSM-III-R, 
DSM-IV-TR, or DSM-5; (2) type of intervention: Culturally Adapted 
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CA-CBT); (3) study design: randomized 
controlled trials (RCTs) and non-randomized controlled trials (non- 
RCTs), including quasi-experimental studies, observational studies, and 
case reports; (4) the control group could receive treatment as usual, 
another active or passive treatment, placebo, waiting list, or no inter­
vention; (5) outcome data was reported on anxiety severity; (6) pub­
lished in English or Indonesian, in peer-reviewed journals with available 
full-text articles. 
We referenced Southeast Asian countries according to the United 
Nations: Brunei Darussalam, Cambodia, Indonesia, Lao PDR, Malaysia, 
Myanmar, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, Timor-Leste, and Viet Nam 
(United Nations, 2022; United Nations Statistics Division, 2022). Post­
traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) was included since PTSD was regarded 
as an anxiety disorder before DSM-5. There were no restrictions on age, 
gender, or any other sociodemographic variables. 
Exclusion criteria included the following: (1) studies using Southeast 
Asian people living outside their home country, for example, refugees; 
(2) CA-CBT combined with pharmacological treatments or other psy­
chological treatments; (3) study protocol without data. 
2.3. Search Strategies 
This study followed Cochrane guidelines for conducting a systematic 
review (Higgins and Green, 2011). We searched in July 2022 and con­
ducted an updated search in June 2023 in the following databases: 
PubMed, PsycINFO, Embase, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled 
Trials (CENTRAL), Garba Rujukan Digital (GARUDA), and Google 
Scholar. Our search strategy included the combination of MeSH terms, 
Thesaurus, and free text words. No limitations were imposed regarding 
the papers’ publishing dates. We sought clarification from the corre­
sponding authors in case of unclarities or missing information in the 
published study. The full search strategy is available in supplementary 
file 1. 
2.4. Screening and data extraction 
Titles and abstracts were screened by two authors (AP and AVI) 
independently. After completing the screening process, the full-text ar­
ticles were collected and checked for their eligibility. Any discrepancies 
were resolved by discussing between two authors (AP and AVI) and 
involving the third and fourth authors (MHN and TKB) if needed. 
Extracted data included the authors, year, country, target population 
and sample size, study design, outcome measures used, intervention, 
type of cultural adaptation, delivery format, comparator, and outcomes. 
2.5. Quality assessment 
Since bias could affect the credibility of the evidence presented, we 
assessed the risk of bias in all studies included. Two reviewers inde­
pendently assessed the risk of bias using the Cochrane risk-of-bias tool 
(RoB 2) for randomized trials (Sterne et al., 2019). For non-randomized 
studies, ROBINS-I was used (Sterne et al., 2016), and Joanna Briggs 
Institute’s (JBI’s) critical appraisal tool was used for studies with case 
report design (Moola et al., 2020). During the assessment process, dis­
agreements between the review authors were resolved by discussion.

following the Cultural Treatment Adaptation Framework (CTAF), which

3. Result 
3.1. Search results 
A total of 1653 records were retrieved from the search. After 
removing duplicates, 1073 records were included for titles and abstract 
screening. Then, we removed 1051 irrelevant records based on title and 
abstract, which led to 22 records that were identified as potentially 
relevant in the full-text review. Finally, seven studies (one RCT, three 
quasi-experimental studies, and three case reports) were found eligible 
for the review for data extraction. The study selection process is pre­
sented in Fig. 1 using the PRISMA flow diagram. 
3.2. 3.2. Study characteristics 
All seven included studies were published between 2007 and 2022 
(two published in 2007–2011; five published in 2017–2022). One study 
was a randomized controlled trial (RCT), three were quasi-experimental 
studies using pre-test and post-test designs, and three were case reports. 
Of the seven studies, three were conducted in Indonesia, two in 
Thailand, one in Singapore, and one in Malaysia. Furthermore, three 
studies focused on PTSD, two on social anxiety disorder, one on panic 
disorder, and one on multiple anxiety disorders. Three studies focusing 
on PTSD were included because they were published before the DSM-5 
was released or referred to DSM IV/DSM-IV-TR, which still included 
PTSD as part of anxiety disorders. For detailed study characteristics, see  
Table 1. 
3.3. Quality assessment 
According to the reviewers’ assessment, one randomized study was 
assessed in the category of some concerns. Three quasi-experimental 
studies were assessed to have a moderate risk of bias, and all three 
case studies were assessed as qualified enough to be included in this 
review. For detailed information, see supplementary file 2.

3.5. Type of cultural adaptations 
Of all studies, four explicitly reported cultural adaptation compo­
nents, while three did not explicitly report the components of adapta­
tions they made. Two studies considered multiple components of 
cultural adaptation (Drmic et al., 2017; Subhas et al., 2021). Of seven 
studies, two modified CBT’s core treatment components (Bryant et al., 
2011; Subhas et al., 2021), and three culturally adapted peripheral 
treatment components (Drmic et al., 2017; Hayati et al., 2018; Subhas 
et al., 2021). Three studies that provide undetailed information (Hayati 
and Tohari, 2022; Pityaratstian et al., 2007; Wahyuningsih and 
Daengsari, 2020) only stated performing adaptation but did not provide 
further information on which specific components or elements of CBT. 
All cultural adaptations were made to improve its acceptability to the 
local communities. One example of cultural adaptation in the CBT 
technique as part of the core treatment components was integrating the 
Buddhist technique of distancing oneself from one’s thoughts into 
cognitive restructuring (Bryant et al., 2011). Other examples focusing on 
peripheral treatment components were translating the language to 
Bahasa Malaysia (Subhas et al., 2021) and changing the example of fear 
in the graded exposure video (Drmic et al., 2017). Detailed information 
on identifying cultural components of the adaptation of the studies using 
CTAF can be seen in Table 2. 
3.6. Cultural adaptation process 
Of seven studies, only two provide information on how the adapta­
tion process was carried out. For more detailed information, see Table 3. 
For the other five studies, we could not identify and synthesize the in­
formation from them due to the limited information provided. 
3.7. Effectiveness of CA-CBT 
This review was not feasible to continue to meta-analysis due to the 
scarcity of trials. This systematic review found only one RCT of CA-CBT 
in Southeast Asia (Bryant et al., 2011), which developed CA-CBT for 
survivors of terrorist attacks in Thailand. The participants who received 
culturally adapted CBT experienced a significantly higher reduction in 
PTSD scores than participants in the control condition who received 
treatment as usual (TAU) consisting of generic counseling. 
4. Discussion 
This study aimed to synthesize the literature on the type and effec­
tiveness of CA-CBT. To the best of the author’s knowledge, this is the 
first study focusing on the Southeast Asian population on CA-CBT for 
anxiety disorders. Based on our inclusion criteria, we found seven 
studies, including one RCT, three quasi-experimental, and three case 
reports. Of seven studies, only four explicitly reported the cultural 
adaptation components they made. Two studies modified CBT’s core 
treatment components, and three conducted cultural adaptations on 
peripheral treatment components – two made cultural adaptations in 
multiple components. Due to the limited number of studies, this study 
could not establish the degree of superiority of CA-CBT over non-CA- 
CBT nor identify the components with the most influence. 
We found interesting examples of cultural adaptation in the core 
component of the treatment, namely incorporating religious and cul­
tural values in the CBT (Bryant et al., 2011; Subhas et al., 2021). 
Implementing cultural adaptation in peripheral components is reported

Based on this review, we conclude that cultural adaptations are 
insufficiently reported in several studies. Three of the seven studies 
provided incomplete information about the cultural adaptation type and 
component (Hayati and Tohari, 2022; Pityaratstian et al., 2007; 
Wahyuningsih and Daengsari, 2020). They did not elaborate on which 
specific CBT components were culturally adapted; they simply indicated 
that performing adaptation was made. Moreover, only two studies 
explicitly reported their framework and explained the adapting process 
in more detail (Drmic et al., 2017; Subhas et al., 2021). 
Several reasons could be indicated why cultural adaptation is not 
sufficiently reported. First, no standard framework has been universally 
agreed upon and applied in research and practice (Rathod et al., 2018). 
In addition, the limited knowledge about cultural adaptation might be 
why several studies made cultural adaptation without a standardized 
framework. As a result, some researchers change or adapt without a 
transparent process. This finding aligns with a review of meta-analyses 
conducted by Rathod and colleagues in 2018 that revealed that from 
12 articles found, most lacked methodological rigor and lacked 
consideration of underlying theory and cultural issues (Rathod et al., 
2018). It becomes a challenge, and further research still needs to be 
developed. On the bright side, it may also be that some researchers have 
argued for the right balance between making new treatments for sub­
groups versus personalization (Bennett and Shafran, 2023). 
The other possible reason is that the authors may not explicitly report 
on the process and content of cultural adaptations. On the one hand, 
standard documentation can lead to high-quality research with 
increased transparency on what, how, and why the cultural adaptation 
was made. Heim et al. (2021) proposed the Reporting Cultural Adap­
tation in Psychological Trials (RECAPT) criteria for reporting cultural 
adaptation in psychological trials. In RECAPT, each trial needs to 
document and report the following criteria: (1) set-up, (2) formative 
research methods, (3) intervention adaptation, and (4) measuring out­
comes and implementation (Heim et al., 2021). 
We could not continue to meta-analysis due to the scarcity of trials. 
This limitation indicates that research on CA-CBT for anxiety in South­
east Asian countries is still much needed. However, one study we found 
in this review (Bryant et al., 2011) reported that CA-CBT showed better 
improvement compared to the control group (treatment as usual 
consisting of generic counseling). This aligns with previous research that 
showed culturally adapted intervention is effective and may even be 
more effective than unadapted intervention (Griner and Smith, 2006; 
Hall et al., 2016) and increases the acceptability of the treatment 
(Chowdhary et al., 2014). A case series study in India has also high­
lighted the need for culturally appropriate and acceptable forms of CBT 
to improve the outcome of CBT – one of them is by maximizing the 
non-specific factors in CBT (Samantaray et al., 2022). 
There is still debate about the extent to which cultural adaptation 
should be made. The critical point is that keeping fidelity to the core 
components while incorporating local values and adaptations is crucial 
(Rathod et al., 2015). Moreover, Bennett and Shafran (2023) stated that 
evidence-based practice needs flexibility in intervention delivery, and 
not all modifications to therapy should be viewed as new treatments but 
instead regarded as meta-competence. Treatments always need adap­
tation to the context and preferences of an individual, also addressing 
individual differences within cultures or countries. Most experts sug­
gested that adapting existing interventions into new contexts is prefer­
able to designing new interventions (Bennett and Shafran, 2023). Such 
an approach would make implementing evidence-based treatments 
across different cultures much more feasible. 
Several recommendations could be drawn from this research. First, 
further research focusing on cultural adaptation needs to be developed 
to get a comprehensive understanding. Second, trials must follow a 
standardized adaptation framework considering theoretical founda­
tions. A clear guideline in reporting criteria is also vital to document and 
report all adaptation processes more transparently. Third, it is important 
to publish a separate article focusing on the cultural adaptation process 
rather than compiling the development in the same article with the 
trials. Explaining the cultural adaptation process in a separate paper 
enhances transparency, leaving more room for other important infor­
mation in the trial paper. 
This review has some strengths and limitations. First, the type of 
anxiety disorders varies, increasing the study’s heterogeneity. This re­
view also could not be continued to meta-analysis, so we could not 
determine the extent to which culturally adapted CBT is more effective 
than non-adapted CBT and which components have the most significant 
impact. Second, the quality of the studies, based on the risk of bias 
assessment, was quite heterogeneous, which may have affected the 
conclusions that can be drawn from the studies. Also, we did not 
consider grey literature in our review and only focused on the paper 
written in English and Indonesian, which might lead to selection bias. 
Despite all the limitations, this paper has several strengths. First, we 
systematically searched databases and used a comprehensive method 
that followed Cochrane guidelines. We only included papers from peer- 
reviewed journals and assessed each paper’s quality and bias using 
standardized tools. Second, although including the paper in Indonesian 
could result in bias, this could make the review sharper in picturing the 
condition in Indonesia. 
In all, this study identified some examples of CBT adaptation in the 
context of anxiety disorders in Southeast Asia. Adaptations included 
both changes to the core components as well as to peripheral details such 
as language and case examples. Further studies will need to show 
whether such structural adaptations indeed lead to better treatment 
outcomes or whether we can invest in “meta-competence”, training 
professionals to adapt the CBT framework to any individual rather than 
developing many local variants. Future studies could also extend the 
region to more than Southeast Asia and cover other psychiatric disorders 
(i.e., depressive disorders) to provide sufficient insight into the cultural 
adaptation of CBT.
